M51/NGC 5195:

  • Other designations: Messier 51b, UGC 8494, PGC 47413, Arp 85
  • Summary:
  • Dwarf galaxy interacting with the Whirlpool Galaxy (aka M51a or NGC 5194
  • Both galaxies are located ~25 million light-years away
  • In the constellation Canes Venatici
  • One of the most famous interacting galaxy pairs
  • Characteristics/observation data:
  • Constellation:        Canes Venatici
  • Right ascension: 13h 29m 59.6s
  • Declination: +47° 15′ 58″
  • Redshift: 465 ± 10 km/s
  • Distance: 25 ± 3 Mly or 7.7 ± 1.0 Mpc
  • Apparent magnitude:        10.5
  • Type: amorphous or SB0 pec
  • Apparent size:        5′.8 × 4′.6
  • Research:
  • History:
  • [20 March 1781] discovered by Pierre Méchain
  • Interaction with the Whirlpool Galaxy:
  • Together, they comprise one of the most noted interacting galaxy pairs
  • 2 galaxies are listed in the Atlas of Peculiar Galaxies as one of several prominent examples of a spiral galaxy with a companion galaxy
  • System was also the subject of very early theoretical investigations into galaxy interactions
  • Connected by a dust-rich tidal bridge
  • Dust in this tidal bridge can be seen silhouetted against the center of NGC 5195
  • Demonstrates that NGC 5195 appears to lie behind the Whirlpool Galaxy
  • Encounter has significantly enhanced the spiral structure of M51
  • [January 2016] BBC science reporter Jonathan Webb 
  • Astronomers have spotted 2 huge waves of gas being burped by the black hole at the heart of a nearby galaxy
  • Swathes of hot gas (detected in X-ray images from NASA's Chandra space telescope) appear to be sweeping cooler hydrogen gas ahead of them
  • Vast, rippling belch is taking place in NGC 5195 (a small, neglected sibling of the Whirlpool Galaxy, 26 million light-years away)
  • Makes it one of the closest black holes blasting gas in this way
  • Findings were presented at the 227th meeting of the American Astronomical Society (AAS) in Florida
  • Dramatic example of feedback between a supermassive black hole and its host galaxy
  • Webb's report cited Marie Machacek, co-author of the study from the Harvard–Smithsonian Center for Astrophysics (CFA)
  • Thinks feedback keeps galaxies from becoming too large
  • Can also be responsible for how some stars form
  • Black holes can create, not just destroy
  • Morphology:
  • As a consequence of the gravitational interaction with the Whirlpool Galaxy, NGC 5195 is highly distorted
  • Classification of its morphology is difficult
  • Sometimes identified as a lenticular galaxy or as an amorphous/irregular galaxy
  • Falls outside the standard morphological classification system
  • Supernova:
  • Supernova 1945A is the only supernova detected within NGC 5195
  • [6 April 1945] supernova was found 10″ NW of the nucleus by Milton L. Humason using the 100-inch (2.5 m) telescope at Mount Wilson Observatory
  • Type I supernova
  • Reached a peak apparent magnitude of 14.0

IC 10:

  • Other designations: UGC 192, PGC 1305
  • Summary:
  • Irregular galaxy in the constellation Cassiopeia
  • Mild starburst galaxy in the Local Group of galaxies
  • Characteristics/observation data:
  • Constellation:        Cassiopeia
  • Right ascension: 00h 20m 17.3s
  • Declination: +59° 18′ 14″
  • Redshift: -348 ± 1 km/s
  • Distance: 2.2 ± 0.2 Mly or 660 ± 60 kpc
  • Apparent magnitude:        10.4 ± 0.2
  • Type: dIrr IV/BCD
  • Apparent size:        6′.8 × 5′.9
  • Notable features: mild starburst galaxy
  • Research:
  • History:
  • [1887] discovered by Lewis Swift 
  • [1935] Nicholas Mayall became the first to suggest that the object is extragalactic
  • Edwin Hubble suspected it might belong to the Local Group of galaxies
  • Status remained uncertain for decades
  • [1962] radial velocity was measured
  • Found to be approaching the Milky Way at ~350 km/s
  • Strengthened evidence for its membership in the Local Group
  • [1996] membership in the group was finally confirmed by direct measurements of its distance based on observations of Cepheids
  • Despite its closeness, the galaxy is rather difficult to study
  • Lies near the plane of the Milky Way
  • Therefore heavily obscured by interstellar matter
  • Relation to Andromeda Galaxy:
  • Apparent distance between IC 10 and the Andromeda Galaxy is about the same as the apparent distance between the Andromeda Galaxy and the Triangulum Galaxy
  • IC 10 may belong to the M31 subgroup
  • Local Group of galaxies:
  • IC 10 is the only known starburst galaxy in the Local Group of galaxies
  • Has many more Wolf–Rayet stars per square kiloparsec (5.1 stars/kpc²) than the Large Magellanic Cloud (2.0 stars/kpc²) or the Small Magellanic Cloud (0.9 stars/kpc²)
  • Although galaxy has a luminosity similar to SMC, it is considerably smaller
  • Its higher metallicity compared to the SMC suggests that star formation activity has continued for a longer time period
  • Evolutionary status of Wolf–Rayet stars suggests that they all formed in a relatively short timespan
  • Ratio between types of Wolf–Rayet stars (WC and WN) in IC 10 is very different from the ratio in other galaxies in the Local Group
  • May be somehow due to the starburst nature of the galaxy
  • Currently, galaxy produces stars at the rate of 0.04–0.08 solar masses per year
  • Gas supply in galaxy can last only a few billion years longer
  • Observations:
  • Observations in far-infrared show that the dust in this mild starburst galaxy is deficient in small grains
  • Hypothesized that any small grains that formerly existed were destroyed by strong ultraviolet radiation in the areas around the hot luminous stars that were formed in the galaxy's recent burst of star formation
  • Peculiarities:
  • Galaxy has a huge envelope of hydrogen gas
  • Apparent size measuring 68′ × 80′, which is far larger than the apparent size of the galaxy in visible light (5.5′ × 7.0′)
  • Visible part of the galaxy seems to rotate in a different direction than the outer envelope
  • Has a H II nucleus

SPT 0346-52:

  • Other designations: 
  • Summary:
  • Galaxy found ~1 billion years after the Big Bang that has one of the highest rates of star formation ever seen in a galaxy
  • Astronomers discovered this stellar construction boom by combining data from Chandra and several other telescopes
  • ALMA revealed this galaxy gave off extreme amount of infrared emission, which could have multiple explanations
  • Chandra's observations ruled out the presence of an actively growing supermassive black hole, bolstering the case of extreme star formation in this galaxy
  • Characteristics/observation data:
  • Category: Normal Galaxies & Starburst Galaxies
  • Right Ascension: 03h 46m 41.13s
  • Declination: -52° 05' 02.11"
  • Constellation:         Horologium
  • Distance: 12.7 billion light-years
  • Research:
  • Distant galaxy undergoing an extraordinary construction boom of star formation
  • 12.7 billion light-years from Earth
  • Astronomers are observing it at a critical stage in the evolution of galaxies, ~1 billion years after the Big Bang.
  • Data from the Atacama Large Millimeter/submillimeter Array (ALMA) revealed extremely bright infrared emission from this galaxy
  • Suggested that the galaxy is undergoing a tremendous explosion of star birth
  • Another possible explanation for the excess infrared emission was the presence of a rapidly growing supermassive black hole at the galaxy's center
  • In this scenario, gas falling towards the black hole would become much hotter and brighter, causing surrounding dust and gas to glow in infrared light
  • To distinguish between these two possibilities, researchers used NASA's Chandra X-ray Observatory and CSIRO's Australia Telescope Compact Array (ATCA), a radio telescope
  • Neither X-rays nor radio waves were detected, so astronomers were able to rule out a growing black hole generating most of the bright infrared light
  • Determined that SPT 0346-52 is undergoing a tremendous amount of star formation, an important discovery for a galaxy found so early in the universe
  • Distorted galaxy image results from a collision between two galaxies followed by them merging
  • Astronomers think such a merger could be the reason why SPT 0346-52 is having such a boom of stellar construction
  • Once the two galaxies collide, gas near the center of the merged galaxy is compressed, producing the burst of new stars seen forming in SPT 0346-52
  • The dark regions represent cosmic dust that absorbs and scatters starlight
  • Composite image
  • X-ray data from Chandra (blue), short wavelength infrared data from Hubble (green), infrared light from Spitzer (red) at longer wavelengths, and infrared data from ALMA (magenta) at even longer wavelengths
  • In the latter case, light from SPT 0346-52 is distorted and magnified by the gravity of an intervening galaxy, producing three elongated images in the ALMA data located near the center of the image
  • SPT 0346-52 is not visible in the Hubble or Spitzer data, but the intervening galaxy causing the gravitational lensing is detected
  • Bright galaxy seen in the Hubble and Spitzer data slightly to the left of the image's center is unrelated to SPT 0346-52
  • There is no blue at the center of the image, showing that Chandra did not detect any X-rays that could have signaled the presence of a growing black hole
  • The ATCA data, not shown here, also involved the non-detection of a growing black hole
  • Data suggests that SPT 0346-52 is forming at a rate of ~4,500 times the mass of the Sun every year, one of the highest rates seen in a galaxy
  • This is in contrast to a galaxy like the Milky Way that only forms about 1 solar mass of new stars per year.
  • Jingzhe Ma (University of Florida) - paper has been accepted for publication in The Astrophysical Journal and is available online
  • References:
  • http://chandra.harvard.edu/photo/2016/spt0346/ 

M81/M82:

  • Other designations:
  • M81: NGC 3031, UGC 5318, MCG+12-10-010, PGC 28630, Bode's Galaxy
  • M82: NGC 3034, UGC 5322, Arp 337, Cigar Galaxy, PGC 28655, 3C 231
  • Summary:
  • M81:
  • Spiral galaxy ~12 million light-years away in the constellation Ursa Major
  • Due to its proximity to Earth, large size, and active galactic nucleus (which harbors a supermassive black hole that is 70 million solar masses), Messier 81 has been studied extensively by professional astronomers
  • Galaxy's large size and relatively high brightness also make it a popular target for amateur astronomers
  • M82:
  • Starburst galaxy ~12 million light-years away in the constellation Ursa Major
  • A member of the M81 Group
  • ~5 times more luminous than the whole Milky Way, and has a center 100 times more luminous than our galaxy's center
  • Starburst activity is thought to have been triggered by interaction with neighboring galaxy M81
  • As the closest starburst galaxy to Earth, M82 is the prototypical example of this galaxy type
  • [21 January 2014] SN 2014J (type Ia supernova) discovered in the galaxy
  • [2014] in studying M82, scientists discovered the brightest pulsar yet known, designated M82 X-2
  • Characteristics/observation data:
  • M81:
  • Constellation:        Ursa Major
  • Right ascension: 09h 55m 33.2s
  • Declination: +69° 3′ 55″
  • Apparent dimension: 26.9 × 14.1 moa
  • Apparent magnitude:        6.94
  • Type: SA(s)ab, LINER
  • Heliocentric radial velocity: −34 ± 4 km/s
  • Redshift: −0.000113 ± 0.000013
  • Galactocentric velocity: 73 ± 6 km/s
  • Distance: 11.8 ± 0.4 Mly or 3.62 ± 0.12 Mpc
  • M82:
  • Constellation: Ursa Major
  • Right ascension: 09h 55m 52.2s
  • Declination: +69° 40′ 47″
  • Redshift: 203 ± 4 km/s
  • Distance: 11.4-12.4 Mly or 3.5-3.8 Mpc
  • Apparent magnitude:        8.41
  • Type: I0
  • Diameter: ~37,000 light-years
  • Apparent size:        11′.2 × 4′.3
  • Notable features: edge on starburst galaxy
  • Research:
  • M81:
  • Discovery:
  • [31 December 1774] first discovered by Johann Elert Bode 
  • Galaxy is sometimes referred to as Bode's Galaxy
  • [1779] Pierre Mechain and Charles Messier reidentified M81
  • Subsequently listed in the Messier Catalogue
  • Dust emission:
  • Most of the emission at infrared wavelengths originates from interstellar dust
  • Found primarily within the galaxy's spiral arms
  • Associated with star formation regions
  • The hot, short-lived blue stars that are found within star formation regions are very effective at heating the dust and thus enhancing the infrared dust emission from these regions
  • Supernova:
  • SN 1993J was discovered on 28 March 1993 by F. Garcia in Spain
  • At the time: 2nd brightest supernova observed in the 20th century
  • Spectral characteristics of the supernova changed over time
  • Initially, it looked more like a type II supernova (a supernova formed by the explosion of a giant star) with strong hydrogen spectral line emission
  • Later, the hydrogen lines faded and strong helium spectral lines appeared, making the supernova look more like a type Ib
  • Variations in SN 1993J's luminosity over time were not like the variations observed in other type II supernova, but did resemble the variations observed in type Ib supernovae
  • Supernova has been classified as a type IIb (a transitory class between type II and type Ib)
  • Scientific results from this supernova suggested that type Ib and Ic supernovae were actually formed through the explosions of giant stars through processes similar to those taking place in type II supernovae
  • Supernova was also used to estimate a distance of 8.5 ± 1.3 Mly or 2.6 ± 0.4 Mpc to Messier 81
  • M81 Group:
  • Messier 81 is the largest galaxy in the M81 Group (a group of 34 galaxies located in the constellation Ursa Major)
  • At ~11.7 Mly or 3.6 Mpc from the Earth, it makes this group and the Local Group (containing the Milky Way) relative neighbors in the Virgo Supercluster.
  • Gravitational interactions of M81 with M82 and NGC 3077 have stripped hydrogen gas away from all three galaxies, forming gaseous filamentary structures in the group
  • Have also allowed interstellar gas to fall into the centers of M82 and NGC 3077, leading to vigorous star formation or starburst activity there
  • Amateur astronomy:
  • Messier 81 is located approximately 10° NW of Alpha Ursae Majoris, along with several other galaxies in the Messier 81 Group
  • Messier 81 and Messier 82 can both be viewed easily using binoculars and small telescopes
  • The 2 objects are generally not observable to the unaided eye, although highly experienced amateur astronomers may be able to see Messier 81 under exceptional observing conditions with a very dark sky
  • Telescopes with apertures of 8 inches or larger are needed to distinguish structures in the galaxy
  • Its far northern declination makes it generally visible for observers in the northern hemisphere
  • It is not visible to most observers in the southern hemisphere, except those in a narrow latitude range immediately south of the equator.
  • M82:
  • Discovery:
  • [31 December 1774] discovered by Johann Elert Bode, together with M81
  • Described it as a nebulous patch
  • About 0.75° away from M81
  • Very pale and of elongated shape
  • [1779] Pierre Mechain independently rediscovered both galaxies
  • Reported them to Charles Messier, who added them to his catalog
  • Structure:
  • Previously believed to be an irregular galaxy
  • [2005] 2 symmetric spiral arms were discovered in near-infrared (NIR) images of M82
  • Arms were detected by subtracting an axisymmetric exponential disk from the NIR images
  • Even though the arms were detected in NIR images, they are bluer than the disk
  • Arms were previously missed due to:
  • M82's high disk surface brightness
  • The nearly edge-on view of this galaxy (~80°)
  • Obscuration by a complex network of dusty filaments in its optical images
  • Arms emanate from the ends of the NIR bar and can be followed for the length of 3 disc scales
  • Assuming that the northern part of M82 is nearer to us, the observed sense of rotation implies trailing arms
  • Starburst region:
  • [2005] the Hubble Space Telescope revealed 197 young massive clusters in the starburst core
  • Average mass of these clusters is around 200,000 solar masses, hence the starburst core is a very energetic and high-density environment
  • Throughout the galaxy's center, young stars are being born 10 times faster than they are inside the entire Milky Way Galaxy
  • In the core of M82, the active starburst region spans a diameter of 500 pc
  • 4 high surface brightness regions or clumps (A/C/D/E) are detectable in this region at visible wavelengths
  • These clumps correspond to known sources at X-ray, infrared, and radio frequencies
  • Consequently, they are thought to be the least obscured starburst clusters from our vantage point
  • M82's unique bipolar outflow (superwind) appears to be concentrated on clumps A/C
  • Fueled by energy released by supernovae within the clumps, which occur at a rate of ~1 every 10 years
  • Chandra X-ray Observatory detected fluctuating X-ray emissions from a location ~600 light-years away from the center of M82
  • Astronomers have postulated that this fluctuating emission comes from the first known intermediate-mass black hole, of roughly 200 to 5000 solar masses
  • M82, like most galaxies, hosts a supermassive black hole at its center with a mass of approximately 3 x 107 solar masses (as measured from stellar dynamics)
  • Unknown object:
  • [April 2010] radio astronomers working at the Jodrell Bank Observatory of the University of Manchester in the UK reported an object in M82
  • Had started sending out radio waves
  • Emission did not look like anything seen anywhere in the universe before
  • There have been several theories about the nature of this object, but currently no theory entirely fits the observed data
  • Has been suggested that the object could be an unusual "micro quasar,"
  • Having very high radio luminosity, having low X-ray luminosity, and being fairly stable, it could be an analogue of the low X-ray luminosity galactic microquasar SS 433
  • However, all known microquasars produce large quantities of X-rays, whereas the object's X-ray flux is below the measurement threshold
  • Object is located at several arcseconds from the center of M82, which makes it unlikely to be associated with a supermassive black hole
  • Has an apparent superluminal motion of 4 times the speed of light, relative to the galaxy center
  • Apparent superluminal motion is consistent with relativistic jets in massive black holes
  • Does not indicate that the source itself is moving above lightspeed
  • Starbursts:
  • M82 is being physically affected by its larger neighbor, spiral M81
  • Tidal forces caused by gravity have deformed M82, a process that started [~100 million years ago]
  • Interaction has caused star formation to increase tenfold compared to normal galaxies.
  • M82 has undergone at least one tidal encounter with M81
  • Resulting in a large amount of gas being funneled into the galaxy's core [over the last 200 Myr]
  • The most recent such encounter is thought to have happened around [2-5 × 108 years ago] 
  • Resulted in a concentrated starburst together with a corresponding marked peak in the cluster age distribution
  • Ran for up to ~50 Myr at a rate of ~10 solar masses per year
  • 2 subsequent starbursts followed [the last ~4-6 Myr ago] 
  • May have formed the core clusters, both super star clusters (SSCs) and their lighter counterparts
  • Stars in M82's disk seem to have been formed in a burst [500 million years ago]
  • Left its disk littered with hundreds of clusters with properties similar to globular clusters (but younger)
  • Stopped [100 million years ago]
  • No star formation taking place in this galaxy outside the central starburst
  • At low levels since [1 billion years ago], on its halo
  • A suggestion to explain those features:
  • M82 was previously a low surface-brightness galaxy, where star formation was triggered due to interactions with its giant neighbor
  • Ignoring any difference in their respective distances from the Earth, the centers of M81 and M82 are visually separated by about 130,000 light-years
  • The actual separation is 300 (+300, -200) kly
  • 2014 Supernova:
  • [21 January 2014, 19:20 UT] a previously unseen star was observed in M82 at magnitude +11.7 by Dr. Steve J. Fossey and 4 students at the University of London Observatory
  • Had brightened to magnitude +10.9 [by 23 January]
  • Examination of earlier observations of M82 found the supernova to be present [on 15/16/17/19/20/22 January], brightening from magnitude +14.4 to +11.3
  • There was no sign of it (to limiting magnitude +17) [on 14 January] 
  • Initially suggested that it could become as bright as magnitude +8.5
  • Well within the visual range of small telescopes and large binoculars
  • Actually peaked at a rather fainter +10.5 [on 31 January 2014] 
  • Preliminary analysis classified it as a young, reddened type Ia supernova
  • The International Astronomical Union (IAU) has designated it SN 2014J
  • One of the closest supernovae to Earth observed in recent decades
  • SN 1993J was at a very similar distance (in M82's larger companion galaxy M81)
  • SN 1987A (in the Large Magellanic Cloud) was much closer
  • SN 2014J is the closest type Ia supernova since SN 1972E

ESO 137-001:

  • Other designations: ESO 137-1 , ESO-LV 137-0010 , LEDA 57532 , PGC 57532
  • Summary:
  • Barred spiral galaxy located in Abell 3627 (Norma Cluster)
  • As the galaxy moves to the center of the cluster, it is stripped by hot gas
  • Creates a 260,000 light-year long tail
  • There is evidence of star formation in the tails
  • [2005] discovered by Dr. Ming Sun
  • Characteristics/observation data:
  • Constellation:        Triangulum Australe
  • Right ascension: 16h 13m 27.305s
  • Declination: -60° 45′ 50.59″
  • Apparent dimension: 1.23′ × 0.55′
  • Type: SBc
  • Research:
  • Galaxy’s fate:
  • Stripping of gas has significant effect on galaxy's development
  • Removes cold gas from the galaxy
  • Shuts down the formation of new stars in the galaxy
  • Changes the appearance of inner spiral arms and bulges
  • Due to the effects of star formation
  • 2 tails:
  • 2 spectacular tails of X-ray emission have been seen trailing behind a galaxy using the Chandra X-ray Observatory
  • Composite image of galaxy cluster Abell 3627 shows:
  • X-rays from Chandra in blue
  • Optical emission in yellow
  • Emission from hydrogen light (H-alpha) in red
  • Optical and H-alpha data were obtained with the Southern Astrophysical Research (SOAR) Telescope in Chile
  • At the front of the tail is the galaxy ESO 137-001
  • The brighter of the 2 tails has been seen before and extends for about 260,000 light years
  • Detection of the second, fainter tail, however, was a surprise to the scientists.
  • X-ray tails were created when cool gas from ESO 137-001 (~10 K) was stripped by hot gas (~100 million K) as it traveled towards the center of the galaxy cluster Abell 3627
  • Observed evaporation of cold gas, which glows at ~10 million K
  • Evidence of gas ~100-1,000 K in the tail was also found with the Spitzer Space Telescope
  • Galaxy clusters are collections of hundreds or even thousands of galaxies held together by gravity that are enveloped in hot gas
  • 2-pronged tail in this system may have formed because gas has been stripped from the two major spiral arms in ESO 137-001
  • Stripping of gas is thought to have a significant effect on galaxy evolution:
  • Removing cold gas from the galaxy, shutting down the formation of new stars in the galaxy, and changing the appearance of inner spiral arms and bulges because of the effects of star formation.
